Integrated Phase Logic Networks

Rachel Grange, Michel Despont, Alexander Eichler, Oded Zilberberg
01.09.2022 â 31.08.2026

Summary

Scientific abstract

Recent technological advances in solid state physics and quantum engineering offer the promise of novel computational hardware. Nanoscale magnetic, electrical, optical, or mechanical devices are being researched and developed as logic units. Their advantages over existing transistor units lie in their low power consumption, their high processing rate, or their added functionality (`beyond transistor'). Furthermore, combining such physical systems on a single chip opens up the possibility of integrated logic architectures that benefit from the respective advantages of different technology platforms to offer new information processing functionalities. For instance, mechanical resonators may serve as on-chip sensors for a particular input, while the processing of the information is performed by faster specialized optical or electrical networks; the computational result could be stored in the polarization of an array of spins.One of the most promising paradigms for novel computational architectures are parallel bimodal networks. Depending on the coupling between the network nodes, the entire system evolves towards a stable `ground state' solution. This behavior can be exploited to find answers to many optimization tasks that are nearly intractable with standard computers that perform operations sequentially. Such tasks include the famous travelling salesman problem, the number partitioning problem, and the MAX-CUT problem, as well as fundamental questions in physics like the ground state of the Ising spin model.Crucial challenges associated with a network of bimodal elements include stability, tunability, and addressability of individual nodes. To overcome these serious issues, physicists have started in recent years to investigate driven nonlinear resonators as logic elements. The nonlinearity bestows upon these resonators several stable states that can be used as elementary information units. One intensively researched type of resonator is the parametric oscillator, to which we refer here as `parametron'. The parametron, which was known since the 1950ies as a building block for computing, experienced a recent revival with the advent of modern nanomechanical, electrical and optical resonators.In our Sinergia project, we propose to design, build and characterize the first parametron-based integrated parallel phase logic networks. We will follow an inclusive approach by combining experimental and theoretical know-how from the fields of optomechanics and nonlinear optics, material science, theory, and information technology. Building up networks from two technology platforms, we will pool resources and share knowledge to maximize our chances for success. The research we propose is located at the confluence of several topical fields, including fluctuating nonlinear dynamics, statistical physics, and neural networks, leading to exciting many-body out-of-equilibrium effects with coupled resonators under time-dependent driving. The composition of our consortium is chosen to reflect all of these different fields and specializations. Together, we will build up a Swiss competence group for parametric networks and will push this nascent technology towards concrete applications. Within our project, we expect to reach important milestones in the demonstration of parallel phase logic networks and perform benchmarking of their performance for optimization tasks in the classical regime, in particular with regards to usage as artificial neural network
